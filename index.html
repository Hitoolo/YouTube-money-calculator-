How Are YouTube Earnings Calculated?

The core formula for YouTube ad revenue is simple. Instead of paying per subscriber or per like, YouTube pays for every 1,000 monetized views. This is tracked using a metric called RPM (Revenue Per Mille).

The Formula

(Views ÷ 1,000) × RPM = Estimated Earnings

Example: 100,000 views with a $5.00 RPM = (100) × $5.00 = $500.00

RPM vs. CPM: What's the difference?

Many creators confuse these two terms. Here is the practical difference:

  • 1
    CPM (Cost Per Mille): This is what advertisers pay YouTube. It is always higher than what you earn.
  • 2
    RPM (Revenue Per Mille): This is what you actually keep. It accounts for YouTube's 45% cut and views that didn't show ads. Always use RPM for your calculations.

4 Key Factors That Affect Your RPM

Geography

Views from "Tier 1" countries (USA, UK, Australia, Canada) pay significantly more than views from developing regions due to advertiser competition.

Niche (Topic)

Finance, Tech, and Real Estate topics have the highest RPMs ($10-$50). Entertainment, Vlogs, and Motivation usually have lower RPMs ($1-$4).

Video Format

Long Form: Higher revenue, allows mid-roll ads. Shorts: Very low RPM ($0.01 - $0.06) but easier to get viral views.

Seasonality

Earnings often peak in Q4 (November & December) as advertisers spend their remaining yearly budgets for the holidays.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much does YouTube pay for 1,000 views? [attachment_0](attachment)

On average, YouTube pays between $1 to $5 per 1,000 views for long-form content. However, this varies wildly. A finance channel might earn $20 per 1,000 views, while a gaming channel might earn $2. YouTube Shorts pay significantly less, typically between $0.01 and $0.06 per 1,000 views.

What are the requirements to monetize on YouTube?

To join the YouTube Partner Program (YPP), you generally need 1,000 subscribers AND either 4,000 valid public watch hours in the last 12 months OR 10 million Shorts views in the last 90 days. You must also reside in an eligible country and have a linked AdSense account.

Does this calculator include YouTube Premium earnings?

This calculator estimates AdSense revenue based on RPM. While RPM often implicitly includes a small portion of Premium revenue in your YouTube Analytics, it does not calculate pure Premium earnings separately, as those are based on how much time Premium members spend watching your content compared to others.

Why is my RPM lower than the calculator shows?

If your actual RPM is lower, check your audience geography (are views coming from countries with lower ad costs?), your content type (is it "made for kids" or age-restricted?), or your video length (videos under 8 minutes cannot have mid-roll ads, which reduces revenue).
